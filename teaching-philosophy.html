Teaching Philosophy

When working for Upward Bound in Providence, RI – a federally funded bridge program which serves primarily first-generation and first-in-family college students – my dedication to transformational pedagogy was born. As a part of the Upward Bound community, I spent three consecutive summers living with and teaching first gen high school students. Committed to their education, their dreams, and their cultures, beyond being a tutor, I was a constant fixture in their residential life as well. I had unique opportunities to build multiple and multilingual literacies in and out of the classroom. During the day, we read novels and plays and formally learned rhetorical strategies, and at night, we held common room freestyles (a form of improvised rap and verses) that created space for similarly sonic and lyrical composition, making use of the tools and linguistic repertoires they carried with them. We composed from their vantage point, challenging barriers between what students have been conditioned to see as writing scenarios and the composing that they do every day. This position taught me three things that irrevocably shaped how I have come to view teaching and writing

First, writing and learning are most powerful when they are communal acts, not individual ones. My classes build community where diverse perspectives are fundamental to the learning environment, especially in a political moment when things are hard. In my classroom, we co-compose and revise community norms, surrounding engagement and participation, late work, generative artificial intelligence usage (gen AI), and peer response. This aims to flatten the presumed hierarchy of the classroom where students can reflect on learning environments that they want to be a part of and take ownership in creating them. The impact of this community-first approach manifests in my teaching evaluations and students’ final course reflections, where students have written: “[Megan’s] modern view of teaching has created a unique culture in the classroom! I say culture, as not every teacher has students who purposefully come to class 30 minutes early to just chat with you and each other…Over the semester, we have built a community of support for one another, and it has tremendously helped me” (student reflection) and “I think the environment that Professor Palmer had set for the semester had been the most beneficial thing in developing my education. She had allowed for me to never feel 'stupid' for any of my work” (course evaluation). As my evaluations show, the values I bring to the learning communities highlights not only their writer’s voice but different aspects of their identities as well.

Second, writing and learning also extend beyond the classroom. Teaching at Johnson and Wales University, a Hispanic Serving Institution and renowned culinary school, where my students wanted to cook, to bake… to not be in a required first-year writing class. One of my students voiced this tension: “Why am I here when I could be working 40 hours a week getting the same information and making money?” Honestly? A good question. While this might cause some writing instructors to get defensive, to fluster, it created a space for us to talk about the various economic pressures they felt, especially as first-generation students of color. It allowed us to talk as well about the ways that writing and composing showed up in kitchens, and how the composition process could extend to the food they made each day.

As a result, my pedagogy frontloads the idea that textuality is everywhere. It is in the daily communication we have with our friends; it is in the literal infrastructure that shapes our day. Students likely will not do the majority of their writing in a classroom after graduation and bring their vast and varied language repertoires in composing situations (like in the classroom) as well as the ones they may not read as composing situations (the kitchen or a restaurant opening). Therefore, writing assignments focus on relevant genres composed in and for audiences they want like their peers, their colleagues, their communities, and potential work environments. I centralize writing theories of genre and discourse community —mastery of particular writing genres or composition methods is not the goal. Gaining the flexibility and awareness to know how to engage with new ones is the goal.

Third, reflective practice is key to both learning and unlearning. In my classes, students’ reflection on any task or experience is just as important as that task or experience itself. I often open a class session with a reflection about how they are doing/feeling/being that day, and every major assignment asks them to reflect on their writing and revision process. In big and small ways reflection is the shared learning environment fabric. The learning reveal is that writing is not an innate ability, but a careful decision-making process based on one’s rhetorical situation.

As such, reflective practice facilitates unlearning Standard American English, instead foregrounding writing effectiveness for communities one wishes to engage. Students point to the ways my courses help they find their voice, writing “[The most beneficial part of this course was] the creativity and learning to defend your position on a topic through different forms of media.” As my course evaluations demonstrate, students understand how language, genre, and discourse communities are power relationships, and that their toolkit can address and even change power of the most unequal sort.

Returning to the student asking, “Why am I here?” Well, we’re all trying to answer that question, and writing is just one of them. My teaching philosophy, therefore, is for students to both learn and unlearn what they know about writing. Upon entering the classroom, they are already adept meaning-makers in their worlds, doing so in a community that supports and cares for them. My pedagogical strategies start from this baseline, reframing the already present student critical literacies with multimodal composition practices, where their own embodied and cultural experiences meld with digital and traditional tools. While these may seem like disparate literacies, they are deeply connected and equally impact how we write every day.
